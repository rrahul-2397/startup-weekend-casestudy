RAHUL RAMESH — PRODUCT DESIGN CASE STUDY 01 / FRANK-U

Startup Weekend · Trinity's Entrepreneurial Society · Oct 2023

A reminder app that nags you like a friend would.

Reminder apps are polite — and polite is easy to ignore. In 54 hours at Startup Weekend, our team of three designed and pitched FrAnK-U: a to-do app whose entire product bet is tone of voice. I owned all of the design — from concept and UI to the landing page we pitched with.

Team

TL;DR — if you read one thing

The habit-app market competes on features. We competed on personality — designing blunt, funny notification copy as the core product, then building the brand, UI, and business model around that single idea. I made every design decision under a hard 54-hour deadline, and the judges' feedback validated the direction.

01

The brief: a startup in a weekend

CONTEXT
& CONSTRAINTS

Startup Weekend is Trinity's Entrepreneurial Society's flagship three-day event: strangers form teams on Friday night, build a business over the weekend, and pitch to a panel of venture capitalists on Sunday. The constraint isn't a side note — it shaped every design decision that follows.

54h total, idea to pitch 1 designer (me) 0 existing brand or research 1 shot in front of VCs

With no time for polished research or iteration cycles, my job was to make the fewest, highest-leverage design bets — and make each one legible to a panel of investors in a ten-minute pitch.

02

The problem: reminders are easy to swipe away

PROBLEM
FRAMING

Busy young adults don't fail to build habits because they lack reminder apps — they already have Google Keep, Todoist, and a native Reminders app. They fail because those reminders are emotionally weightless. A grey notification that says "Gym — 7:30am" costs nothing to dismiss.

Nobody ignores a friend who texts "get up, you said you'd go." Everybody ignores a notification that says "Reminder: Gym."

So we reframed the problem. Instead of competing with productivity tools on organisation, FrAnK-U competes on accountability: an app that talks to you the way a blunt friend does — hence the name, "Frank". The AI angle was in generating that persuasive, personal copy per user, rather than canned reminder text.

03

Three design bets, made fast

KEY
DECISIONS

With 54 hours, I couldn't explore broadly — I had to commit. These are the three bets I made, why I made them, and what each one cost.

Bet 01

Tone of voice is the product, not a garnish

The differentiator lives in the notification, not the app. So I designed the notification copy first and treated the app as the place you configure Frank, not the place you live.

Every other reminder app

Reminder: Gym
You have 1 task due today

FrAnK-U

Get off your ass. Time for GYM. 🏋️
Third snooze, huh? Bold strategy.

The trade-off I accepted Blunt copy will alienate some users. We scoped the tone to be user-controlled — which led directly to Bet 02.

Bet 02

A "nagging interval" the user sets themselves

Persistence is only charming when it's consented to. I designed a nagging interval control into the profile — you tell Frank how many times to hassle you before he gives up. It turns the app's most annoying behaviour into its most personal setting.

Why it matters This one control is the difference between "motivational" and "notification spam" — and it gave the pitch a concrete answer to the inevitable "won't people just uninstall this?" question.

Bet 03

Ruthlessly minimal capture: two fields, one button

Todoist wins on power features; we couldn't out-feature it in a weekend and shouldn't try. Creating a reminder in FrAnK-U asks exactly two things — what and when — because our users are people who abandon complex tools, not people who crave them.

What I cut Projects, labels, priorities, sub-tasks. Every feature we didn't build was a deliberate scope decision, not a gap.

04

The designs

UI +
BRAND

I designed 13 screens in Figma across onboarding, capture, and notifications, plus the marketing site. The visual language — a single loud coral on white, mono-spaced brand type, an emoji as the logo — was chosen to feel cheap, cheeky and confident, like the copy. A refined, tasteful UI would have contradicted the product's voice.

Being honest: this was a concept sprint, not a shipped product. There are no retention numbers, and the designs never met a real user. What this project does show is the thing hackathons uniquely test — judgment under constraint: what to design first, what to cut, and how to make design decisions carry a business argument.

  1. Design the moment of truth first.Starting with the notification — not the home screen — meant every screen after it had a purpose. I now start every project by asking where the product actually earns its keep.
  2. A strong voice is a design system.Once "blunt friend" was defined, decisions got faster: empty states, button labels, even the merch designed themselves. Personality scaled better than any component library could in 54 hours.
  3. Pitching is a design skill.VCs don't review Figma files; they buy narratives. Presenting to a panel taught me to frame design choices as business bets — a habit I've kept for every design review since.
  4. What I'd do differently.I'd spend two of those 54 hours testing the notification copy on strangers at the venue. The tone was our biggest bet, and we defended it with conviction when we could have defended it with evidence.

FrAnK-U taught me that the strongest design decisions are the ones you can defend to an investor — not just to another designer.
